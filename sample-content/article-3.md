# Coordination Failures at Scale

*Smart people, good intentions, shared goals — and still nothing happens. The mystery isn't why individuals fail. It's why groups of capable people fail so reliably, and in such predictable ways.*

---

## The Puzzle

Here is a thing that happens constantly: a group of intelligent, well-meaning people identifies a serious problem. They agree on its severity. They agree, roughly, on what would help. And then they do not do that thing, or they do it too slowly, or they do a version of it that's been diluted past usefulness by the process of getting everyone to agree.

Climate change is the obvious example, but it's almost too big to think about clearly. Start smaller. Open source software projects that everyone depends on but nobody funds. Academic fields where the replication crisis has been known for a decade and the incentive structures haven't changed. Cities where everyone agrees housing is too expensive and the zoning laws stay the same. Companies where the dysfunction is visible to every employee and the org chart doesn't move.

The puzzle isn't that people are stupid or malicious. The puzzle is that the failure mode is so consistent. Something structural is happening.

## The Prisoner's Dilemma Is Too Simple

The standard explanation is game theory. Collective action problems, free riders, the tragedy of the commons. Everyone would be better off if everyone cooperated, but each individual is better off defecting, so everyone defects, and the commons gets destroyed.

This is true as far as it goes. But it doesn't explain why the problem persists even when people know about it, even when they've read the game theory, even when they genuinely want to cooperate. The prisoner's dilemma assumes that players can't communicate and can't build trust. Real coordination failures happen among people who talk to each other constantly and trust each other reasonably well.

Something else is going on.

Part of it is what you might call the legibility problem. For cooperation to happen, people need to be able to see what others are doing. In a small group, this is easy — you can watch. At scale, it becomes nearly impossible. How do you verify that the other 195 countries are actually reducing emissions? How do you know if the other open source maintainers are putting in their hours? You can't watch. You have to trust proxies, and proxies are gameable.

The moment a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. This is Goodhart's Law, and it applies everywhere. Carbon credits get gamed. Citation counts get gamed. Diversity metrics get gamed. The proxy becomes the goal, and the actual goal drifts.

## The Veto Problem

There's another failure mode that gets less attention: the asymmetry between blocking and building.

In most collective decision-making systems, it's much easier to stop something than to start it. A single objection can derail a proposal. A single holdout can prevent consensus. A single lawsuit can freeze a project for years. This isn't an accident — it's often a feature, designed to protect minorities from majorities. But it has a side effect: it makes the status quo extremely sticky.

The people who benefit from the current arrangement don't need to win arguments. They just need to prevent the other side from winning. This is a much lower bar. You don't need a coherent alternative vision. You just need lawyers, or lobbyists, or the ability to raise procedural objections indefinitely.

Climate negotiations are a masterclass in this. The countries most responsible for emissions don't need to convince anyone that climate change isn't real. They just need to ensure that any binding agreement has enough carve-outs and escape hatches to be effectively voluntary. The form of cooperation is achieved. The substance isn't.

## Why Smart People Make It Worse

Here's the uncomfortable part: intelligence doesn't help as much as you'd think, and sometimes makes things worse.

Smart people are better at constructing justifications for whatever they were already inclined to do. They're better at identifying edge cases that complicate any proposed solution. They're better at anticipating how a proposal might be gamed, which is useful, but can also become a reason to never propose anything. The perfect becomes the enemy of the good, and the smart person who identified seventeen ways the plan could fail feels vindicated when it does.

There's also a status problem. In groups of smart people, being the one who raises the objection is often higher-status than being the one who proposes the solution. Proposals can be wrong. Objections are almost always defensible. The incentive structure rewards skepticism over commitment.

This is why so many high-IQ institutions — think tanks, academic departments, certain tech companies — produce a lot of analysis and not much action. The analysis is genuinely good. The action is genuinely hard. And the people doing the analysis are rewarded for the analysis, not the action.

## What Actually Works

When coordination does happen at scale, it tends to share a few features.

First, there's usually a crisis that makes the cost of inaction suddenly legible. The ozone layer problem got solved partly because the science was clear and partly because the economic interests of the chemical industry were manageable. The coordination happened fast, by international standards. The threat was concrete and near-term enough that the veto players couldn't hold out indefinitely.

Second, successful coordination usually involves reducing the number of players who need to agree. The Paris Agreement involves 195 countries, which is why it's mostly aspirational. The actual emissions reductions that have happened came from a handful of large economies making domestic policy changes. Smaller coalitions, clearer accountability.

Third, and maybe most importantly: the most durable coordination mechanisms are the ones that change the underlying incentives rather than trying to override them. Carbon taxes, if they work, work because they make the externality visible in the price. You don't need everyone to care about the climate. You just need the price signal to be accurate.

## The Honest Assessment

None of this is particularly hopeful. The structural features that make large-scale coordination hard — legibility problems, veto asymmetries, status incentives, the gap between individual and collective rationality — are not going away. They're features of how humans organize at scale.

What's worth holding onto is that coordination failures are not random. They fail in predictable ways, which means they can be studied, and sometimes designed around. The question is whether the people with the power to change the structures have any incentive to do so.

Usually, they don't. Which is itself a coordination problem.
