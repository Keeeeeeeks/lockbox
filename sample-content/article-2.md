# The Attention Tax

*Every time you open an app, something is taken from you. Not your data — that's a separate conversation. Something harder to quantify and harder to get back.*

---

## The Transaction You Never Agreed To

There's a moment, maybe thirty seconds after you unlock your phone, when you realize you don't remember why you picked it up. You were going to do something. Check a flight time, maybe, or send a message. Instead you're watching a video of a dog failing to catch a frisbee, and the original intention has dissolved completely.

This is the attention tax in its purest form. Not the time you spent — time is at least measurable. The tax is the cognitive residue: the slight disorientation, the broken thread of whatever you were actually thinking about before the phone appeared in your hand. That thread doesn't always come back.

We talk about attention as if it's a renewable resource, something that replenishes overnight like sleep. But anyone who has spent a day in back-to-back meetings, or a week doom-scrolling through a news cycle, knows that attention has a texture to it. It can be coarse or fine. It can be directed or scattered. And the scattered kind is much harder to reassemble than we like to admit.

The digital economy runs on this asymmetry. Platforms are extraordinarily good at capturing attention. They are not particularly interested in returning it.

## What the Market Actually Prices

The phrase "attention economy" has been around long enough to feel tired, but it's worth sitting with what it actually describes. An economy is a system for allocating scarce resources. Attention is scarce. Therefore, there's a market for it.

The problem is that this market has a pricing problem. When you watch an ad, the advertiser pays the platform. You, the person whose attention was consumed, receive nothing except the content that was used to capture you in the first place. The content is the bait. Your attention is the product. You are not a participant in this transaction — you're the commodity being traded.

This would be fine, maybe, if the exchange were transparent. If every time you opened Instagram there was a small notification: "This session will cost approximately 14 minutes of focused attention and leave you with a mild sense of inadequacy. In exchange, you'll see 47 photos, 12 of which you'll find genuinely interesting." You might still make the trade. But you'd make it consciously.

Instead, the entire design philosophy of these systems is oriented toward making the trade invisible. The infinite scroll removes the natural stopping point. The notification badge creates urgency where none exists. The algorithmic feed is tuned not for your satisfaction but for your continued engagement, which are related but not the same thing.

## The Honest Market

What would an honest market for attention actually look like?

Start with disclosure. If a platform is going to sell your attention to advertisers, you should know the rate. Not in vague terms ("we use your data to show relevant ads") but in actual numbers. This session generated $0.003 in advertising revenue. Your attention, priced at market rates, is worth about a third of a cent per minute. That's not an insult — it's just the number. Knowing it changes the transaction.

Then there's the question of consent. Not the fake consent of a 47-page terms of service that nobody reads, but something more like a genuine negotiation. You want my attention for the next twenty minutes? What are you offering? Some platforms have experimented with subscription models that remove ads, which is a start. But it doesn't address the deeper issue, which is that even ad-free platforms are designed to maximize time-on-site, and time-on-site is not the same as value delivered.

The honest version might look more like a gym membership than a casino. You pay a fixed amount. You get access to the equipment. The gym has no particular incentive to keep you there longer than you want to be. You leave when you're done. The relationship is clean.

## What Gets Lost

The thing that worries me isn't the time. Time is recoverable, sort of. What worries me is the quality of thought that becomes unavailable when attention is constantly fragmented.

There's a particular kind of thinking that requires sustained focus — not just concentration, but the willingness to sit with a problem long enough for it to become interesting. The kind of thinking that produces good writing, or good science, or good decisions. It doesn't happen in the gaps between notifications. It requires something closer to boredom: the uncomfortable state of having nothing immediately stimulating to look at, which forces the mind to generate its own material.

Boredom has been nearly eliminated as a human experience. Every waiting room, every commute, every moment of transition is now fillable with content. This seems like a pure improvement until you notice that the people who produce the most interesting ideas tend to be the ones who still have long stretches of unstructured time. The correlation isn't coincidental.

The attention tax isn't just about the minutes lost to scrolling. It's about the thoughts that never happened because the conditions for them were never created.

## The Reckoning

None of this is new information. The books have been written, the documentaries made, the congressional hearings held. And yet the systems continue to operate more or less as designed, because the incentives haven't changed. Platforms are rewarded for engagement. Engagement is maximized by capturing attention. Attention is most easily captured by exploiting psychological vulnerabilities. The logic is airtight.

What might actually change it is harder to say. Regulation is slow and tends to address symptoms rather than structures. Individual willpower is real but limited — you can delete the apps, but the apps will be back in some form, and they'll be better at what they do.

Maybe the honest market for attention is less a policy proposal than a thought experiment. A way of asking: if you had to pay for what you're consuming, and if the platform had to pay for what it's taking, would this exchange still make sense? For some of it, yes. For a lot of it, probably not.

That gap between "yes" and "probably not" is where the tax lives.
